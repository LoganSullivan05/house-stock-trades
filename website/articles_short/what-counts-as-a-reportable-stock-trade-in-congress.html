What Counts as a Reportable Stock Trade in Congress?

Introduction

Not every financial move made by a member of Congress needs to be disclosed. The STOCK Act sets clear thresholds and criteria for what constitutes a reportable stock trade. This article outlines what trades must be reported and why.

Minimum Thresholds

Only trades with a value exceeding $1,000 are considered reportable. This applies to purchases, sales, or exchanges of stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and other securities. Smaller transactions fall below the reporting threshold and are not disclosed.

Types of Reportable Assets

  • Individual stocks and equities
  • Bonds and fixed-income securities
  • Mutual funds and ETFs
  • Options, derivatives, and other complex instruments

Assets held in certain retirement accounts, such as TSPs or employer-sponsored 401(k)s, are generally not subject to transaction reporting. Additionally, jointly held assets managed by third-party advisors may fall outside the scope if the member lacks direct control.

Reporting Deadlines

Once a reportable trade occurs, a Periodic Transaction Report (PTR) must be filed within 30 to 45 days. These filings include the transaction date, asset type, and a value range but not the exact dollar amount.

Conclusion

Understanding what qualifies as a reportable trade helps voters assess the transparency and behavior of their elected officials. While the STOCK Act provides a foundation, public engagement is key to accountability.
