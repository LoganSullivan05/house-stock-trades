How to Read a Congressional Periodic Transaction Report

Introduction

Periodic Transaction Reports (PTRs) provide a snapshot of financial activity by members of Congress. Learning to read these reports helps voters evaluate potential conflicts of interest and gain insight into the financial behavior of elected officials.

What Is a PTR?

A PTR is a disclosure form required by the STOCK Act. It must be filed within 30–45 days of a qualifying transaction, such as a stock or bond purchase over $1,000. These reports are accessible to the public and can be found on official government websites.

Key Sections of a PTR

  • Transaction Date: When the trade occurred.
  • Owner: Indicates whether the asset belongs to the member, spouse, or dependent child.
  • Asset Name: The stock, bond, or fund traded.
  • Transaction Type: Purchase, sale, or exchange.
  • Amount: Disclosed in a range (e.g., $1,001–$15,000).

Common Terms and Abbreviations

You may encounter shorthand such as "SP" (spouse) or "DC" (dependent child). Also, keep in mind that dollar values are always listed in ranges to maintain privacy.

Reading Between the Lines

Patterns in transaction timing, industry concentration, or synchronized trades across multiple members may suggest strategic behavior. PTRs provide clues, but deeper analysis is often needed to draw conclusions.

Conclusion

Understanding PTRs is an essential skill for citizens interested in political accountability. While these reports don’t tell the whole story, they serve as a valuable tool for transparency and civic engagement.
